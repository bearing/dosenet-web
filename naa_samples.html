This page represents a summary of the elemental composition, specifically trace metals, of kelp, fish, and other sea life along the west coast of the Americas.


Background

Within the Berkeley Radwatch program, we have generally focused on measurements to study - mostly naturally occurring - radioactivity in samples that are part of environment and food (see Gamma Sampling Data). This was done by so-called passive means employing very sensitive high-purity germanium (HPGe) gamma-ray spectrometers. We can extend these measurements using nuclear technology to look beyond the naturally occurring radioactive materials in samples and study the presence of non-radioactive elements that can be made radioactive through irradiation with neutrons in a process called neutron activation.

This Neutron Activation Analysis (NAA) provides very sensitive means to determine the elemental composition, specifically the concentrations of trace metals in a wide range of objects and samples. We have initiated measurements to study the composition of some of the samples we have previously analyzed with passive counting, including kelp, fish, and other sea life along the west coast of the Americas. The analysis of the NAA data enables the quantification of trace metals such as mercury or arsenic in these samples down to below ppm concentrations, dependent on the specific element. The NAA results also provide the opportunity to compare trace metal concentrations in samples across different locations and over time. In addition, we can compare the results to other measurements and to regulatory limits to see whether the concentrations we observe can potentially pose a health hazard.

NAA is a non-damaging and relatively simple analysis method that allows for very accurate and precise measurements of chemical composition. Samples are irradiated with a high neutron flux, which activates all neutron capturing isotopes within the sample. These activated isotopes then decay, emitting gamma rays at characteristic energies specific to that isotopes, similar to the passive method using HPGe spectrometers. Based on the known natural abundance of the observed isotopes we can estimate the total elemental composition of the corresponding element in the sample.

Most activated isotopes usually have half-lives roughly a day long, which means that these isotopes will remain radio-active long enough to acquire a measurement with good statistical accuracy. While these half-lives allow for accurate measurements, they can also present a safety hazard due to radiation exposure. However, we do not expect to deal with hazardous isotopes nor activities large enough to pose significant radiation hazards.

We hope that these new measurements will help to illustrate some of the powerful methods for understanding our environment utilizing nuclear technologies and improve awareness of some of the non-radioactive materials we are exposed to in our environment and food which could potentially provide a health hazard.

Summary of Results

This results demonstrate the effectiveness of this analysis method, allowing us to determine the elemental composition of many sea samples. Using a relatively simple activation method, we were able to determine the concentrations of thirteen different elements, including arsenic, mercury, and bromine. Normally, it's difficult to chemically measure bromine, for example, due to interference from other halogens like chlorine. Neutron activation occurs regardless of chemical properties since the only relevant property is the neutron capture cross section.

Sea Fish

Table 2: Reference sample is a Greenland halibut. Sodium concentrations from USDA nutrition facts.



Table 2 shows the results for Wild King Salmon and Halibut. The Wild King Salmon was found to not have any sodium, arsenic, or bromine. This is not surprising since it is a freshwater fish which is usually caught from a river. Salmon would not acquire sodium or any of its compounds as it swims upstream. It was also found to have less selenium than the halibut, but significantly more cesium.

Halibut tend to live their lives as bottom dwellers, mostly living on the sea floor. As a result of this, they tend to accumulate minerals or metals that are present in the sea floor, arsenic being one of them. This halibut in particular has a about 30% more arsenic than the reference. This halibut was caught in the west coast and purchased from a local fish market. Arsenic is present in the earth's crust as well as in the seawater at typical concentrations of 1.5 ppb [6]. It is possible that this accumulates within the halibut as it lives in the environment and consumes other arsenic containing sea creatures.

Trying to determine mercury concentrations presents a challenge when selenium is also present. Normally, we'd try to look for Hg-203 by using the 279 keV gamma ray. Hg-203 is used since it's the longest lasting activated isotope, with a half-life of ~47 days. However, if selenium is present in the sample, specifically Se-74, then there would be a 279 keV gamma ray from activated Se-75. Se-75 also emits three other gamma rays that can be used to confirm its presence whereas Hg-203 emits only one. The selenium in our samples is confirmed through the additional gamma-ray peaks. Using those peaks to esitmate the expected count-rate in the peak at 279 keV, the concentration of mercury can be exstimated.

Seaweed

Table 3: Reference sample is a Japanese wakame, Calcium and Sodium concentrations from the USDA nutrition facts.



The Wakame was found to not have significant levels of arsenic and generally had less sodium and bromine than the sea palm. Wakame is known to be an invasive species of seaweed on the west coast. Depending on its origins, it may not have accumulated as much arsenic over its lifetime as the sea palm. Sea palms tend to grow on rockier shores, which may explain this discrepancy. Other than the notable difference in arsenic, the concentrations of other trace metals are similar.

Sea Shells

Table 4: Similar to water or soil samples, the precence of trace metals in sea shells can serve as an indicator for their precence in the local environment.



The high level of Ca and Sr in sea shells is indicative of their bone-like composition, and therefore not surprising. Though we do not typically consume shells, they can serve as a marker for the precence of higher than normal levels of traces metals in the environment, with different sensitivities to such elements than kelp.

Toxicity Data

Table 5: Drinking water limits are generally set by the EPA, with details provided through the Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry (ATSDR) (see Ref. 14-20). Injestion limits are estimates based on various sources (see Ref. 21-31).


Most elements and chemicals we consume can be toxic at high enough levels, or cause long-term side effects if ingested at high rates over a long period of time. Table 5 lists the EPA and/or FDA limits for many of the elements found in the samples discussed here, and references for all of these are listed below. Many of these are essential trace elements but toxic in excess. For example, there are adverse health effects related to deficiencies in selenium (Se), zinc (Zn), calcium (Ca), and iron (Fe), while chronic exposure to extremely high levels from food and water can also cause adverse health effects or even prove fatal in the case of Iron poisoning (Ref. 30, 32, 24, 26 respectively).

Of particular interest here are trace elements considered dangerous to humans even at very low levels, specifically arsenic (Ar) and mercury (Hg). In the case of Ar, the main concern is inorganic arsenic. Long term, exposure to higher levels of inorganic arsenic can increase the risk of some cancers. The FDA tests specific foods, mainly rice and juices, which are most likely to absorb inorganic arsenic through soil and ground water contamination, and sets limits dependending and the expected rate of consumption [33]. The limit stated in Table 5 corresponds to the FDA limit set on inorganic arsenic in infant rice cereal, and extreme case as exposure in children is particular concern. However, to assess the results shown here, it is important to note that there are several forms of Ar, which NAA cannot distinguish between. The ATSDR says “The predominant dietary source of arsenic is seafood” and “While seafood contains the greatest amounts of arsenic, for fish and shellfish, this is mostly in an organic form of arsenic called arsenobetaine that is much less harmful. Some seaweeds may contain arsenic in inorganic forms that may be more harmful.” Thus, it is unlikely that the Ar levels stated here for sea fish are cause for concern.

The presence of cesium (Cs) in some of the fish and kelp samples may be of concern to some people. While it may be easy to think that this is either Cs-137 or Cs-134 from radioactive waste, the measured cesium radiation comes from activated Cs-133, which is stable and naturally occurring. However, cesium plays no biological role in the human body, but can replace potassium since it's in the same chemical group. Similarly, rubidium (Rb) can also mimick potassium (K) in biochemical processes in the body. Because of the substitution for potassium in the body, each has what is called a biological half-life, 70 days for Cs and 45 for Rb, meaning the body will remove half of the cesium or rubidium ingested within 70 and 45 days respectively [38]. While neither element is particularly toxic, an extreme excess of either Cs or Rb can therefore lead to a potassium deficiency, causing hypokalemia, arrythmia, and acute cardiac arrest. That said, there are no EPA or FDA limits on either element. The limits listed in Tabel 5 are based on studies related medical applications for cesium chloride [25] and rubidium chloride [28] which found serious health effects for Cs intakes at the 6 g/day and no adverse effects from Rb intakes as high as ~500 mg/day.

Strontium (Sr) is also better known as a bi-product of nuclear weapons testing, which produces the radioactive isotope Sr-90. However, again the Sr observed here is from the naturally occurring stable isotope. While stable Sr does not pose a significant health risk, it will be absorbed by the body as if it were calcium (Ca), getting absorbed into the bone, and has a biological half-life of roughly 18 years. The limit quoted in Table 5 corresponds to the concentration of Sr in leafy vegetables, one of the main contributors to dietary Sr consumption [31]. The average intake rate is about 2 mg/day. This is not generally an issue for adults, however in children unusually high levels of Sr intake can lead to bone growth problems, though typically only if their diet is low in protein and calcium [19,37].

Where do we go from here?

Neutron activation has shown to be a useful tool in determining compositions. A total of 13 elements were identified for kelp, along with 8 for fish and seaweed and 12 for the seashells. Some of these elements would be very difficult to measure otherwise either due to their chemical interactions or very small quantities.

Future work will involve analyzing more species of fish and measuring for compositional differences between farmed and wild fish. It would be of interest to see whether farmed fish have higher concentrations of any impurity elements like arsenic.
